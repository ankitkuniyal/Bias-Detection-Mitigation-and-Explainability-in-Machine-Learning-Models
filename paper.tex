\begin{abstract}
Machine learning models deployed in high-stakes environments often inherit and amplify historical biases present in training data. This study presents a comprehensive experimental framework for bias detection, mitigation, and explanation across three diverse datasets: Adult Income, COMPAS Recidivism, and Statlog German Credit. Fairness is quantified using Statistical Parity Difference (SPD), Equal Opportunity (EO), Disparate Impact (DI), and Average Odds Difference (AOD), while performance is assessed using Balanced Accuracy and the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) to account for class imbalance. We compare Reweighting (pre-processing), Adversarial Debiasing (in-processing), and Threshold Adjustment (post-processing) against standard machine learning and Artificial Neural Network (ANN) baselines. Explainable AI (XAI) via SHAP interaction analysis is employed to mathematically isolate how sensitive attributes interact with non-sensitive proxies. Results demonstrate that Threshold Adjustment achieves the most consistent reduction in disparities. Conversely, Adversarial Debiasing exhibits catastrophic variance on small datasets, necessitating multi-run averaging. SHAP interaction analyses successfully expose indirect biases, emphasizing the critical need to integrate multi-metric fairness evaluation, mitigation strategies, and XAI to ensure transparency in automated decision-making.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Machine learning (ML) models are increasingly deployed to automate high-stakes decision-making across domains like hiring, credit approval, and criminal justice \cite{R1, R4, R11}. While offering unprecedented efficiency, an extensive body of research indicates these systems can produce inherently unfair results that disproportionately penalize protected demographic groups \cite{R18, R20}. If historical data is tainted by societal inequalities, deep learning architectures do not simply reflect these biases—they often magnify them into the objective function, leading to untrustworthy predictions \cite{R7, R9, R12}.

Mitigating these disparities introduces complex operational challenges. Studies consistently demonstrate an inherent mathematical tension—the fairness-accuracy trade-off—where enforcing strict parity inevitably degrades predictive performance \cite{R6, R14, R19}. Furthermore, identifying bias macroscopically is insufficient; non-sensitive attributes frequently act as hidden proxies for protected characteristics \cite{R17}. To uncover these hidden sources, Explainable AI (XAI) methods, such as SHAP \cite{R21}, are required to transform models into transparent systems, acknowledging inherent mathematical attribution limits \cite{R15}.

To bridge the gap between theoretical fairness metrics and practical interpretability, this study implements a \textbf{fairness-aware machine learning framework}. By evaluating an integrated pipeline across three distinct public datasets, this paper provides a transparent, multi-metric evaluation of pre-, in-, and post-processing interventions against rigorous neural network baselines, uniquely leveraging SHAP interactions to isolate indirect proxy biases.

\section{Literature Survey}
\subsection{Origins of Bias and Performance Tensions}
The mathematical and societal foundations of algorithmic fairness trace back to formulations by Mehrabi \textit{et al.} \cite{R20}. Recent literature categorizes bias into data-driven origins, algorithmic constraints, and deployment loops \cite{R3, R7}. Deep learning frequently exacerbates these anomalies by optimizing purely for historical patterns without fairness constraints \cite{R7, R11}. 

Enforcing strict fairness metrics reveals deep-seated mathematical incompatibilities. Rabonato and Berton illustrate that optimizing simultaneously for predictive parity and group fairness remains mathematically impossible under unequal base rates \cite{R6}. This fairness-accuracy trade-off is an unavoidable challenge where maximizing statistical parity inherently dampens global metrics like accuracy or $F_1$-scores \cite{R14, R19}.

\subsection{Mitigation Strategies and XAI}
Bias mitigation is historically partitioned into pre-processing, in-processing, and post-processing \cite{R13}. Pre-processing (e.g., sample reweighting) alters the underlying data distribution \cite{R12}. In-processing methodologies, including adversarial debiasing, penalize the internal loss function of neural networks, though they are notoriously unstable on small datasets \cite{R9}. Post-processing alters individual decision thresholds to balance true positive rates without re-training \cite{R13}, frequently providing more reliable corrections in volatile environments like criminal recidivism \cite{R8}.

Furthermore, models legally blinded to sensitive attributes often utilize non-protected features as mathematically viable proxies \cite{R17}. To diagnose this, the research community relies on SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) \cite{R21}. While SHAP interaction analysis successfully isolates multi-variable proxy dependencies, recent proofs by Bilodeau \textit{et al.} establish mathematical impossibility theorems for universal attribution guarantees, warning practitioners that explanations require statistical caution \cite{R15}.

\section{Dataset Description}
To rigorously evaluate the generalizability of our framework, we selected three publicly available datasets spanning diverse domains.

\subsection{Adult Income Dataset}
Extracted from the 1994 US Census, this dataset predicts whether an individual earns more than \$50,000 annually. It contains demographic, educational, and occupational variables. \textit{Gender} is isolated as the protected attribute to evaluate demographic parity.

\subsection{COMPAS Recidivism Dataset}
Derived from ProPublica's analysis of the US criminal justice system, the task is a binary classification determining whether a defendant will reoffend within two years. \textit{Race} (Caucasian vs. Non-Caucasian) is designated as the protected attribute.

\subsection{Statlog German Credit Dataset}
A standard benchmark in the financial sector classifying individuals into "Good" or "Bad" credit risks based on 20 financial and demographic attributes. \textit{Age} is designated as the protected attribute, with individuals over 25 defined as the privileged group.

\section{Methodology}
Our methodology is structured into a five-phase pipeline explicitly designed to address class imbalance and the stochastic nature of algorithmic interventions.

\subsection{Baseline Models and Performance Metrics}
We established Logistic Regression (LR) and Random Forest (RF) as baseline architectures. These specific models were selected to explicitly contrast a highly interpretable, parametric linear decision boundary against a highly complex, non-parametric ensemble method capable of learning deep non-linear interactions. To ensure a scientifically valid evaluation of deep learning interventions, we also train a baseline Artificial Neural Network (ANN). To rigorously evaluate performance on class-imbalanced data, we rely on \textbf{Balanced Accuracy} and the \textbf{Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC)}.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Optimization and Reproducibility}
To ensure reproducibility and rigorous baseline comparisons, hyperparameter optimization was performed for each classifier using a 5-fold grid search on the training splits. For the Random Forest (RF) models, we tuned the number of estimators (evaluated across $\{25, 50, 100\}$) and maximum depth (evaluated across $\{5, 10, \text{None}\}$), selecting $50$ estimators and a maximum depth of $10$ to prevent overfitting on smaller tabular datasets. The baseline Artificial Neural Network (ANN) and the Adversarial Debiasing predictor share an identical multilayer perceptron architecture consisting of two hidden layers with $64$ and $32$ neurons, respectively. We optimized the training using the Adam solver, setting the initial learning rate to $0.001$, and batch sizes to $128$. Training ran for $200$ epochs for the baseline ANN, and was constrained to $50$ epochs for the Adversarial Debiasing runs to mitigate the computational cost of the multi-seed variance tracking. All models are initialized across fixed random seeds (42 to 46 for multi-run stochastics) to guarantee numerical reproducibility.

\subsection{Fairness Metrics}
Fairness is quantified using a multi-metric approach:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Statistical Parity Difference (SPD):} Disparity in positive prediction rates between groups.
    \item \textbf{Disparate Impact (DI):} Ratio of positive prediction rates (0.80--1.25 bounds the legal "80\% Rule").
    \item \textbf{Equal Opportunity Difference (EOD):} Difference in True Positive Rates (procedural fairness).
    \item \textbf{Average Odds Difference (AOD):} Average of FPR and TPR differences.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Bias Mitigation Toolkits and Techniques}
To ensure reproducibility, our fairness interventions utilize implementations from IBM AI Fairness 360 (AIF360) and Microsoft Fairlearn.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reweighting (Pre-processing):} Utilizing AIF360, this calculates the probability of assigning specific protected class outcomes and assigns fairness-aware weights to instances. By mathematically neutralizing historical imbalances, this method is fundamentally designed to reduce \textbf{Demographic/Statistical Parity (SPD)} disparities.
    \item \textbf{Adversarial Debiasing (In-processing):} Utilizing AIF360, this employs a generative adversarial network. To isolate the debiasing effect, our primary predictor mirrors our baseline ANN: a Multilayer Perceptron with two hidden layers (64, 32 neurons), ReLU activation, and standard cross-entropy loss. The adversarial penalty forces the primary predictor to unlearn latent correlations with the protected attribute, explicitly targeting reductions in \textbf{Statistical Parity (SPD)}. To document inherent stochastic volatility, results report the arithmetic mean and standard deviation ($\pm$) across five independent initializations.
    \item \textbf{Threshold Adjustment (Post-processing):} Implemented via Fairlearn's \textit{ThresholdOptimizer}, utilizing a Calibrated Equalized Odds constraint. By fine-tuning probabilistic decision thresholds, this algorithm is mathematically guaranteed to target and minimize \textbf{Equal Opportunity (EO)} disparities by equalizing True Positive Rates across groups.
\end{itemize}

\section{Experimental Results and Discussion}
The complete quantitative results, including predictive performance and fairness metrics, are summarized in Table \ref{tab:combined_results}.

\subsection{Adult Income Dataset and the Fairness-Accuracy Trade-off}
In the Adult dataset, the baseline models exhibit severe demographic disparity. Crucially, the baseline standard ANN exhibits high bias (SPD = 0.172 $\pm$ 0.006), confirming that the reduction in bias observed during Adversarial Debiasing (SPD = 0.057 $\pm$ 0.025) is strictly attributable to the adversarial penalty, not merely the deep learning architecture.

As illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:metrics_adult}, while Threshold Adjustment successfully neutralized procedural fairness (EO = 0.020), it provides a severe manifestation of the fairness-accuracy trade-off. The Matthews Correlation Coefficient dropped precipitously from 0.594 in the baseline RF to 0.388 under Threshold Adjustment. To force Equal Opportunity, the post-processing algorithm indiscriminately flipped negative predictions to positive for the unprivileged group, severely inflating the False Positive Rate. This confirms that achieving procedural parity in deeply entrenched socio-economic datasets often comes at the severe cost of global predictive utility.

When mitigation is applied, Threshold Adjustment emerges as the most stable intervention. \subsection{Statlog German Credit Dataset and Stochastic Volatility}
The German Credit dataset, comprising only 1,000 instances, exposes the raw mathematical sensitivity of complex in-processing mitigation algorithms. 

While Reweighting and Threshold Adjustment effectively collapsed disparities, Adversarial Debiasing demonstrated catastrophic stochastic instability (Figure \ref{fig:metrics_german}). Across multiple random initializations, the Adversarial model yielded a massive standard deviation in outcome fairness (SPD = 0.179 $\pm$ 0.169). This variance proves that generative adversarial networks require massive batches of data to calculate stable gradients. On small tabular datasets, the adversary fails to learn a generalized representation of the protected attribute, leading to volatile updates that completely destabilize the primary predictor.

Traditional fairness metrics successfully quantify global outcome disparities but fail to mathematically explain their origins. \subsection{Mathematical Limitations of Tree SHAP}
While these visualizations provide profound diagnostic utility, it is critical to acknowledge the mathematical limitations of post-hoc feature attributions. Tree SHAP interaction values approximate game-theoretic Shapley values by relying on path-dependent routing. When non-sensitive proxy features are highly collinear with protected attributes, Tree SHAP can arbitrarily distribute the interaction weight, potentially underestimating proxy bias severity. Furthermore, linear SHAP formulations are mathematically blind to true concurrent interaction effects. Therefore, these XAI visualizations serve as powerful diagnostic approximations rather than cryptographic proofs of model logic.

Ablating these proxy features resulted in minor drops in predictive accuracy across all datasets (ranging from -0.001 to -0.025), indicating that these features contain predictive signal. However, contrary to the naive expectation that removing demographic proxies automatically improves fairness, the Statistical Parity Difference (SPD) actually increased (e.g., +0.060 for COMPAS and +0.019 for German Credit). This counter-intuitive behavior occurs because the Random Forest model, when deprived of explicit proxies, relies on other complex, non-linear feature interactions to reconstruct the target variable, which can inadvertently exacerbate demographic disparities. This empirical proof demonstrates that proxy ablation is insufficient for debiasing and reinforces the need for explicit mathematical interventions like Threshold Adjustment.

\section{Discussion and Future Directions}
A key conceptual limitation of the current algorithmic auditing landscape is the reliance on single-axis demographic attributes. In this study, we evaluated protected attributes (sex, race, age) in strict isolation. However, in real-world socio-technical environments, bias rarely operates on a single dimension. Rather, it operates intersectionally, compounding disparities across overlapping identities. For example, within the high-stakes COMPAS dataset, evaluating "race" and "gender" in isolation masks the compounded punitive bias experienced by specific intersectional subgroups, such as Black women. Recent literature, such as frameworks proposed by Scarone \textit{et al.} at AIES 2025 \cite{R12}, emphasizes that bias mitigation must account for these multi-dimensional intersectional definitions to prevent the inadvertent exaggeration of secondary disparities. Future work will expand this framework by utilizing techniques like intersectional subgroup reweighting to scale our evaluation pipeline to multi-axis identities, providing more rigorous formal fairness guarantees.

\section{Conclusion}
This research presents a comprehensive, multi-metric framework for detecting, mitigating, and explaining bias in machine learning models. Experimental evaluation across three diverse datasets demonstrates that fairness-oriented methods can reduce demographic disparities while introducing unavoidable, and sometimes severe, trade-offs in predictive performance (MCC degradation). Threshold Adjustment consistently achieved the most robust fairness improvements, while Adversarial Debiasing demonstrated catastrophic stochastic sensitivity on small tabular datasets. Additionally, our novel causal ablation study proves empirically that proxy ablation (removing features like age or workclass) often paradoxically increases demographic disparities (e.g., +0.060 SPD). This serves as a powerful argument against "naive" debiasing (simply removing sensitive proxy features), as the model actively compensates by exploiting alternative complex correlations. The findings emphasize the absolute necessity of integrating explicit mathematical interventions, variance reporting, and XAI techniques to develop truly transparent and accountable systems.
